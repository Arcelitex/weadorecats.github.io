Why Indoor Cats Have Different Food Needs

Indoor cats live a fundamentally different life from outdoor cats. They are not chasing prey, climbing trees, or running long distances. Most of the day they spend sleeping on the couch, lounging in a sunny spot, or watching birds through the window. This is lovely, but it also means they burn far fewer calories than outdoor cats.

The result is that indoor cats are far more prone to weight gain. Studies suggest that over 50% of pet cats in the United States are overweight or obese, and indoor cats are a big part of that statistic. Extra weight in cats is not just a cosmetic issue. It puts serious strain on their joints, increases the risk of diabetes, causes liver problems, and shortens their life.

Beyond weight, indoor cats also tend to swallow more fur because they groom themselves more frequently without the natural shedding that outdoor activity provides. This leads to hairballs, which range from mildly annoying to genuinely uncomfortable for your cat. A good indoor cat food will address both of these issues.

The short version: Indoor cats need fewer calories, more fiber to help with hairballs, and high protein to keep their muscles lean even without much exercise. Keep that in mind and you are already ahead of most cat owners.

What to Actually Look for on the Label

You do not need a nutrition degree to read a cat food label. You just need to know what the important bits mean.

Protein should be first

Look at the first ingredient. It should be a named meat source like chicken, turkey, salmon, or beef. Not "meat by-products," not "poultry meal," and definitely not corn or wheat as the first ingredient. Cats are obligate carnivores, meaning their bodies are built to run on animal protein. A food that leads with a grain or a vague "meat" source is not doing your cat any favors.

Watch the carbohydrate content

Cats have very little need for carbohydrates. Unlike humans, they do not have the digestive enzymes to process large amounts of starch efficiently. High-carb foods can cause blood sugar spikes and contribute to weight gain and diabetes over time. Many dry cat foods are surprisingly high in carbs because grains are cheap and help the kibble hold its shape.

Taurine must be present

Taurine is an amino acid that cats cannot produce on their own. Without enough taurine in their diet, cats develop serious heart problems and can go blind. Any reputable commercial cat food will have taurine added, but it is worth confirming it is on the ingredient list.

  • Named meat as the first ingredient (chicken, turkey, salmon, beef)
  • Taurine listed in the ingredients
  • No artificial colors, flavors, or preservatives
  • AAFCO statement confirming the food is "complete and balanced"
  • Moderate fat content for weight management
  • Added fiber (beet pulp or psyllium) for hairball control

Wet Food or Dry Food? The Honest Answer

This is probably the most debated topic in cat nutrition, and people get surprisingly passionate about it. The honest answer is that both have a place in a good diet, and the best approach for most cats is actually a combination of the two.

Dry food is convenient, cost-effective, and dental health claims aside, it is a perfectly fine base for most cats. The problem is that it is low in moisture and often higher in carbohydrates than cats ideally need. If your cat eats only dry food their entire life, many will do just fine, but others will develop urinary issues or kidney disease over time partly from chronic mild dehydration.

Wet food is closer to what a cat would naturally eat in the wild. It is high in protein, high in moisture, and generally lower in carbohydrates. The downsides are cost and the fact that it needs to be eaten quickly before it dries out or spoils.

Our recommendation: Use a quality dry food as the base of your cat's diet and add a small portion of wet food once or twice a day. You get the convenience and dental benefits of dry food combined with the hydration and nutritional benefits of wet food. Most vets agree this is a sensible middle ground.

How Much Should You Feed Your Indoor Cat?

This is where a lot of cat owners go wrong, and it is not their fault. Free feeding (leaving food out all day) works for some cats who self-regulate well, but a surprising number of cats will simply eat whenever food is available regardless of whether they are actually hungry.

As a general starting point, most adult indoor cats need somewhere between 200 and 300 calories per day depending on their size and activity level. Check the feeding guide on your specific food's packaging, but treat those numbers as a starting point rather than gospel. Every cat is different.

The best way to know if you are feeding the right amount is to feel your cat's ribs. You should be able to feel them easily without pressing hard, but you should not be able to see them. If the ribs are hard to find under a layer of padding, your cat is probably getting too much food. If they are prominent and visible, increase the portions slightly.

  • Most indoor cats need 200 to 300 calories per day
  • Split meals into two sittings rather than one large meal
  • Measure portions with a kitchen scale for accuracy
  • Adjust portions based on your cat's body condition every few months
  • Factor treats into the daily calorie total
